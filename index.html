About Me

Hello, I'm Nathan! Welcome to my portfolio page! Here, you can learn all sorts of things about who I am and what I do. Let's get started!

I was the kid that always wanted to explore something new, like trying to make plasma by microwaving a grape. Now, instead of breaking microwaves, I'm striving to create without the destructive side effects. When the time came for college, I ended up choosing to study computer engineering, where most of my accidental unwanted side effects could be fixed with a 'Ctrl+C' or a 'git revert', but where I still have the opportunity to make objects light up, process outputs of ultrasonic sensors, and just interact with the physical world with the things I build (even if a few LEDs or resistors are sacrificed in the process). Now, I'm starting my third and final year for my Bachelor's degree, and I'm on track to complete my Master's degree in computer engineering by Spring of 2022.

My current interest resides in finding and understanding the applications of theoretical principles that I am learning about in my courses. I find abstract notions to be wonderful to think about, I believe that the true beauty of these theoretical concepts is how they can be applied to our daily lives. Two specific topics that I am currently diving in to are cryptography and electronic linear systems. In crypto, I can continue to constructively break things. Since the whole point of cryptography is to make systems that can provide a secure line of data flow between users, finding ways to break cryptographic objects (or failing to do so) helps with determining what methods of cryptography should be used in the real world. In electronic linear systems, I've dealt with the modeling of operations on signals using Fourier properties. While these systems are modeled with sets of equations, they can be applied to real world situations, such as modulating a talk radio signal to a certain frequency, which can be demodulated by a radio tuning in to that frequency.

In my spare time, I enjoy reading scary stories, pondering different philosophical schools of thought as I strive to discover more about myself, and collecting Pokemon in hopes that I one day will complete the Pokedex, even if The Pokemon Company designs more and more Pokemon every few years.

UCSD Triton

I am currently a student at UC San Diego, majoring in Computer Engineering. In the field of computer science, I have taken classes on topics such as Software Engineering, Data Structures, and Algorithm Analysis. Moreover, I have taken courses in the field of electrical engineering, such as Analog Design and Linear Systems.

In the summer after my second year at UCSD, I participated as a STEP Intern at Google. As a part of this internship, I worked with two other interns in developing a tool for extracting important phrases and their corresponding timestamps from YouTube videos. The motive behind this project is to allow for fast searching through YouTube videos of long lectures as we begin to determine whether an online education is a viable option.

I personally developed much of the backend pipelining of data through Natrual Language Processing (NLP) services. The main challenge behind this project lies in the maintenance of phrase to timestamps mapping as we send text data through the NLP services. We recognize that the longer the text we parse in, the more accurate the NLP service will find words that may be considered important in its context. However, as we increase the amount of text that we pass in, we lose the timestamp accuracy. Manipulating the constraints of this problem was one of my main focuses, as I experimented with both the theoretical mathematics behind this problem as well as real video data.

I worked under the guidance of Dr. Siavash Mirarab on measuring the correctness of an error detection tool for multiple sequence alignments of phylogenetic data. Over the course of my research, I introduced specified errors to correct datasets to determine the effectiveness of the tool under different conditions and designed a pipeline to test the algorithm on hundreds of input datasets. In the end, I was able to present my research findings at UCSD's Summer Research Conference of 2019.

I am currently finalizing my results from the data that I have collected with my scripts. As of now, we have tested the effectiveness of error correcting on three simulated datasets as well as one real dataset of multiple sequence alignments. The conditions that I have tested include varying lengths of error, the percentages of rows that contain error, as well as variations in the algorithm parameters themselves. We are currently writing a paper in hopes to introduce the algorithm and publish the results.
